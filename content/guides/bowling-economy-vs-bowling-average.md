## What economy rate shows

Economy rate is about control. It answers: how many runs does this bowler concede per over? In limited-overs cricket, this can be extremely important because every run affects the target or chase.

A bowler with an economy of 5.50 in a high-scoring match may have done an excellent job. A bowler with 8.00 in a low-scoring match may have struggled. Context matters.

## What bowling average shows

Bowling average is about wickets in relation to runs conceded. It answers: how many runs does this bowler concede for each wicket? If a bowler concedes 100 runs and takes 5 wickets, the average is 20.00.

If wickets are zero, normal bowling average cannot be calculated because division by zero is not possible. That does not automatically mean the bowler was poor; they may have bowled economically without taking wickets.

## How they can disagree

A bowler can have a low economy but a high average if they control runs without taking many wickets. Another bowler can have a good average but expensive economy if they take wickets while conceding boundaries.

For example, a defensive spinner may bowl 10 overs for 35 runs and no wickets. The economy is excellent, but there is no bowling average. A strike bowler may take 3 wickets for 60 runs. The average is 20.00, but the economy may be expensive depending on overs bowled.

## Format differences

In T20 cricket, economy is often highly valued because innings are short. In longer formats, wicket-taking can matter more because dismissing batters is the path to winning. ODI cricket sits between the two, requiring both control and wicket threat.

## Role matters

New-ball bowlers, middle-over spinners, and death bowlers have different jobs. A death bowler may concede more runs because batters attack every ball. A middle-over bowler may be expected to control singles and build pressure.

## Best reading

Use both metrics together. Economy tells you about run control. Average tells you about wicket cost. Add dot ball percentage, boundary frequency, extras, and match phase for a fuller view.

## Simple comparison example

Bowler A bowls 4 overs for 20 runs and takes no wickets. The economy is 5.00, which shows strong control, but a normal bowling average cannot be calculated because wickets are zero. Bowler B bowls 4 overs for 36 runs and takes 3 wickets. The economy is 9.00, but the bowling average is 12.00.

Neither spell can be judged by one number alone. Bowler A may have built pressure that helped another bowler take wickets. Bowler B may have broken the innings open despite conceding more runs. The match role decides how the numbers should be valued.
