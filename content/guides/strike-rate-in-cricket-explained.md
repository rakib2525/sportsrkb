## Strike rate by format

In T20 cricket, a high strike rate can change a match quickly. In one-day cricket, the desired strike rate may depend on the innings phase. In longer cricket, a lower strike rate can still serve the team if the batter is protecting wickets or wearing down the bowling.

## Why balls faced matters

Strike rate is based on balls, not overs. A batter who scores 30 from 15 balls has a strike rate of 200.00 because they scored two runs per ball on average.

## Balance with average

Strike rate shows speed, while average shows consistency by dismissal. A complete batting picture usually needs both numbers.

## Match situation matters

A high strike rate is valuable when a team needs quick runs, but it is not the only goal. A batter rebuilding after early wickets may accept a lower strike rate for a few overs. A finisher may need a much higher strike rate because fewer balls remain.

In T20 cricket, strike rate is often discussed heavily because every ball is precious. In ODI cricket, a batter may start slower and accelerate later. In long-format cricket, patience can be part of the role.
