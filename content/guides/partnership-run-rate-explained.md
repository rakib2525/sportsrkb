## What a partnership is

A partnership is the runs scored while two batters are at the crease together. It starts when the second batter arrives and ends when one of the two is dismissed, retires, or the innings ends. Partnerships are important because cricket is not only about individual scores. A pair can rebuild pressure, rotate strike, or launch an attack together.

Partnership run rate focuses on the scoring speed of that stand. It tells you whether the pair moved the innings forward quickly or slowly.

## The formula

Partnership run rate = partnership runs divided by decimal overs.

The important detail is overs conversion. Cricket overs are written in balls, not normal decimals. A stand lasting 5.4 overs lasted 5 overs and 4 balls, which is 34 balls. In decimal overs, that is 34 divided by 6, or 5.6667 overs.

## Simple example

Suppose two batters put on 60 runs in 6 overs. The partnership run rate is 10.00. That is a strong scoring pace in many short-format situations.

Now suppose another pair puts on 60 runs in 12 overs. The partnership run rate is 5.00. The same number of runs were scored, but the match effect is different because the scoring took twice as long.

## Why context matters

A partnership run rate cannot be judged without context. If a team is 15 for 3, a partnership of 50 at 5 runs per over may be excellent because it protects the innings. If a team needs 100 from 60 balls, the same rate may be too slow.

Match format also matters. In T20 cricket, a partnership may need to score quickly. In a longer match, survival and batting time can be valuable.

## Reading a chase partnership

During a chase, partnership run rate can show whether the batting pair is keeping up with the required rate. If the required rate is 8.50 and the partnership is scoring at 9.00, the chase is moving in the right direction. If the partnership is scoring at 5.50, pressure is building unless wickets are being protected for a planned finish.

Use the required run rate calculator alongside partnership run rate when reviewing a chase.

## Reading a first-innings partnership

When batting first, partnership run rate helps identify momentum shifts. A quiet opening stand may be acceptable if the pitch is difficult. A middle-over stand that scores at 8 or 9 can set up a strong final total.

Coaches can use this number to review whether batters rotated strike well, found boundaries, or became stuck against certain bowling types.

## Common mistakes

The most common mistake is treating 7.5 overs as 7.5 decimal overs. In cricket, 7.5 means 7 overs and 5 balls, which is 47 balls, or 7.8333 decimal overs. Using 7.5 directly gives a slightly wrong run rate.

Another mistake is counting only the runs scored by one batter. Partnership runs include both batters plus extras scored while they are together, depending on scorecard convention.

## Practical use

Use partnership run rate to understand phases, not to blame one batter without context. A stand can be slow because of pitch, bowling, wickets lost, or a deliberate plan. It can also be fast because both batters took risks.
