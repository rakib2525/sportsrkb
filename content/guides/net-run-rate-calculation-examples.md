## Example 1: equal overs, positive NRR

Imagine Team A scores 160 runs in 20 overs. Its scoring run rate is 160 divided by 20, which equals 8.00 runs per over. The opponent scores 140 runs in 20 overs. The conceded rate is 140 divided by 20, which equals 7.00.

The simple NRR estimate is 8.00 minus 7.00. That gives +1.00. This is positive because Team A scored faster than it allowed the opponent to score.

This is the easiest type of example because both innings use the same number of overs.

## Example 2: equal overs, negative NRR

Now reverse the situation. Team A scores 140 in 20 overs, while the opponent scores 160 in 20 overs. Team A's scoring rate is 7.00. The opponent scoring rate is 8.00.

The NRR estimate is 7.00 minus 8.00, which equals -1.00. This is negative because Team A conceded runs faster than it scored them.

Negative NRR does not automatically mean a team is eliminated. It simply means the rate comparison is currently poor.

## Example 3: chase completed early

Suppose Team A chases 121 runs in 15 overs. Its scoring rate for that chase is 121 divided by 15, which equals 8.07. If the opponent scored 120 in 20 overs, the conceded rate is 6.00.

The simple rate difference is 8.07 minus 6.00, which equals +2.07. Finishing a chase early can help because the batting side uses fewer overs to reach the target.

This is why chase speed can matter in tournament tables, especially near the end of a group stage.

## Example 4: partial overs

Partial overs are where mistakes often happen. Suppose a team scores 90 runs in 10.3 overs. That means 10 overs and 3 balls, or 63 balls. In decimal overs, 63 divided by 6 equals 10.5.

The scoring rate is 90 divided by 10.5, which equals 8.57. If someone incorrectly divides by 10.3, the answer becomes 8.74, which is not the correct cricket calculation.

Always convert partial overs through balls first.

## Example 5: comparing two teams on points

Two teams may both finish on 8 points. Team A has a simple NRR of +0.300. Team B has a simple NRR of +0.050. If the tournament uses NRR as the tiebreaker, Team A ranks higher.

However, if the tournament uses another tiebreaker first, such as wins or head-to-head result, the table may not follow NRR immediately. That is why tournament rules matter.

## Example 6: all-out caution

If a team is all out before using its overs, official NRR rules may count the full over quota rather than the overs actually faced. This can change the official number. Manual examples are still useful for learning, but they may not match the official table exactly.

For club or friendly tournaments, organizers should write down how all-out innings are handled before the event starts. That avoids confusion later.
