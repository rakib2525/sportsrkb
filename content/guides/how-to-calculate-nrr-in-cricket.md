## Why NRR matters

Net run rate is most useful in league-style cricket where two or more teams can finish on the same number of points. A team with a strong NRR has usually scored quickly, restricted opponents well, or done both across the matches that count.

## How to read the result

A positive NRR means the team has scored faster than it has conceded. A negative NRR means the opposite. A number close to zero suggests the team has been roughly balanced in scoring and conceding rates.

## Practical note

Use NRR as a planning estimate unless you are following an official table. Tournament playing conditions can change how all-out innings, shortened games, abandoned matches, and revised targets are counted.

## How teams think about NRR

Teams usually focus on winning first because points are the main table currency. NRR becomes more important when qualification could depend on tiebreakers. A large win can help NRR, while a heavy defeat can damage it, but chasing NRR too aggressively can create unnecessary risk.

For example, a chasing team may want to reach a target quickly if wickets are in hand. If the chase becomes risky, securing the win is usually more important than trying to finish a few overs earlier. In a league table, two points from a win are normally more valuable than a small NRR improvement.
