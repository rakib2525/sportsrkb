## What run rate tells you

Run rate is a simple measure of scoring pace. If a team scores 100 runs in 10 overs, its run rate is 10.00 runs per over. It is useful during an innings because it shows whether the batting side is moving quickly or slowly.

Run rate does not say anything about the opponent's scoring. A team may score at 8 runs per over, but if the opponent scores at 9 runs per over, the team is still behind in the rate comparison.

## What net run rate tells you

Net run rate adds the defensive side of the game. It compares the rate at which a team scores with the rate at which it concedes. That is why a strong bowling performance can improve NRR even if the batting total is not huge.

In a tournament, NRR often reflects a team's overall dominance or struggle. Big wins tend to help. Heavy defeats tend to hurt. Close matches may have a smaller effect.

## Why the difference matters

During one innings, fans usually focus on current run rate and required run rate. During a points-table discussion, NRR becomes more important because it can separate teams with equal points.

For example, two teams may both have six points. One team may have won comfortably and lost narrowly, while the other won narrowly and lost heavily. The first team may have the stronger NRR even with the same points total.

## Official rule caution

Simplified NRR calculators are useful for planning and understanding the concept. Official tables may handle all-out innings, reduced-over matches, and abandoned matches according to competition rules. Always check the playing conditions for formal standings.

## Easy way to remember

Run rate asks: How fast are we scoring?

Net run rate asks: How fast are we scoring compared with how fast opponents score against us?

That comparison is the key difference.
