## Mistake 1: treating overs like normal decimals

The most common net run rate mistake is using cricket overs as if they were normal decimal numbers. Cricket notation is different. The number after the dot is balls in the current over, not tenths of an over. That means 7.5 overs is 7 overs and 5 balls, not seven and a half overs. In decimal form, 7.5 cricket overs equals 47 balls, or 7.833 decimal overs.

This matters because NRR is built from run rate, and run rate is runs divided by overs. If the overs are wrong, every later calculation is wrong. A small mistake can become important in a tight table.

Use a balls-to-overs converter when in doubt. Convert cricket overs into legal balls, then divide by 6 for decimal overs. That is the safest way to avoid confusion.

## Mistake 2: using only one innings for tournament NRR

Another common mistake is calculating NRR from one innings and treating it as the full tournament value. One match can affect NRR, but tournament NRR usually uses all counted matches. A team might have a strong NRR after one big win, then lose some of that advantage after a narrow win or heavy defeat.

For a basic manual estimate, you can compare scoring rate and conceded rate for a match or group of matches. But if you are trying to match an official table, you need the exact tournament rules and all relevant innings data.

## Mistake 3: ignoring conceded rate

Some beginners think NRR is only about how fast a team scores. It is not. NRR compares scoring rate with conceded rate. A team can score quickly but still have a poor NRR if opponents score even faster against it.

For example, scoring at 8.00 runs per over sounds strong. But if the opponent scores at 9.20, the simple rate difference is negative. Bowling and fielding are just as important as batting in NRR.

## Mistake 4: forgetting all-out rules

All-out innings can be handled differently in official NRR calculations. In many tournament conditions, if a team is bowled out before using its full overs, the full quota may still be counted for NRR purposes. This can make official values different from simple manual estimates.

That does not make a simple calculator useless. It still helps you understand direction and approximate impact. But for official standings, always check competition rules.

## Mistake 5: comparing NRR before checking points

NRR is important, but points usually decide the table first. A team with more points normally ranks above a team with fewer points, even if the lower-points team has a better NRR. NRR becomes most important when teams are level on points or when the tournament rules specify it as a tiebreaker.

Read a table in order: points, matches played, wins or other tiebreakers, then NRR depending on rules.

## Mistake 6: assuming NRR predicts future results

NRR describes what has happened in counted matches. It does not predict future match outcomes. A team with strong NRR can still lose the next match. A team with weak NRR can still qualify if it wins enough games and other results help.

Use NRR as a table and planning tool, not a forecast.

## Practical checklist

Before trusting an NRR number, ask four questions. Were overs converted correctly? Were both scoring and conceded rates included? Is this a simple estimate or an official table calculation? Are points and tournament rules being considered first?

If the answer to all four is yes, your NRR reading is much more likely to be useful.
