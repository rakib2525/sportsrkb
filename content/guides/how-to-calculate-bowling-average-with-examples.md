## What bowling average tells you

Bowling average is a wicket-based bowling metric. It answers a direct question: how many runs does a bowler concede for each wicket they take? If a bowler gives away 200 runs and takes 10 wickets, the average is 20.00.

Lower is usually better because the bowler is taking wickets without allowing too many runs. But cricket roles matter. A bowler used mainly to stop runs in the middle overs may have a different profile from an attacking strike bowler.

## The formula

Bowling average = runs conceded divided by wickets taken.

Only runs conceded by the bowler are used. Wickets taken are wickets credited to that bowler. Fielding dismissals such as run outs are not normally credited as bowler wickets unless the scorecard rule says so for a specific dismissal type.

## Simple example

Suppose a bowler concedes 96 runs across a short tournament and takes 4 wickets. The bowling average is 96 divided by 4, which equals 24.00. That means the bowler has conceded 24 runs for each wicket.

If another bowler concedes 96 runs but takes 8 wickets, the average is 12.00. The second bowler took wickets more often for the same runs conceded.

## Zero-wicket cases

If a bowler has conceded runs but taken no wickets, a standard bowling average cannot be calculated. You cannot divide by zero. A calculator should show a friendly warning rather than returning an incorrect value.

This does not mean the bowler was useless. A bowler might bowl tightly, create pressure, or support wickets at the other end. To understand that role, also check economy rate and dot ball percentage.

## Bowling average versus economy rate

Economy rate is runs conceded per over. Bowling average is runs conceded per wicket. A bowler can have a good economy and a modest average if they keep runs down but do not take many wickets.

For example, a bowler may bowl 8 overs for 32 runs and take 1 wicket. The economy is 4.00, which is controlled. The average for that spell is 32.00, which shows only one wicket was taken.

## Bowling average versus strike rate

Bowling strike rate measures balls bowled per wicket. If a bowler takes 5 wickets in 120 balls, the bowling strike rate is 24. That means a wicket every 24 balls on average.

Bowling average adds the runs side of the story. A bowler could take wickets often but concede too many boundaries. Looking at average, strike rate, and economy together gives a better view.

## Format matters

In shorter formats, bowlers may accept a higher risk to take wickets. In longer formats, building pressure across spells can matter more. A number that looks excellent in one format may be ordinary in another.

Local cricket also varies widely. Ground size, pitch condition, ball quality, and scoring accuracy can all affect bowling figures. Use the number as a guide, not an absolute label.

## Practical tracking tips

Keep a simple bowling record: overs, runs conceded, wickets, dot balls, and extras if possible. Use the bowling average calculator for the wicket metric, the economy rate calculator for run control, and the bowling strike rate calculator for wicket frequency.

When those numbers point in the same direction, the bowler's role is easier to understand. When they conflict, the details of the spell become important.
