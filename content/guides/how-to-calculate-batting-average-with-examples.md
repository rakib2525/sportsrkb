## What batting average means

Batting average is one of the oldest and simplest cricket performance measures. It tells you how many runs a batter scores for each time they get out. A higher average usually means the batter is making useful runs and avoiding dismissals.

The idea is simple, but beginners often get confused by not-outs. Cricket does not divide total runs by innings played. It divides total runs by dismissals. That difference matters because a batter can finish not out and still add runs to the total.

## The basic formula

The formula is:

Batting average = total runs divided by dismissals.

If a batter has 300 runs and 10 dismissals, the average is 30.00. If another batter has 300 runs and 6 dismissals, the average is 50.00. Both scored the same number of runs, but the second batter was dismissed fewer times.

## How not-outs work

Suppose a batter plays five innings: 40, 25 not out, 10, 60 not out, and 15. The total runs are 150. The batter was dismissed in the 40, 10, and 15 innings, so dismissals are 3. The average is 150 divided by 3, which is 50.00.

This is why not-outs are valuable. They can help a team finish an innings or chase and they also affect average. However, a not-out average should still be understood with context. A batter with two not-out innings may not have enough innings for a strong judgment.

## Example with several innings

Imagine a batter has these innings in a local tournament: 32, 8, 45 not out, 0, 27, and 18 not out. Total runs are 130. Dismissals are 4 because two innings were not out. The batting average is 130 divided by 4, which equals 32.50.

If someone incorrectly divides by 6 innings, they would get 21.67. That is not the standard cricket average. The correct denominator is dismissals.

## What if dismissals are zero?

If a batter has scored runs but has never been dismissed, normal division cannot produce an average. For example, 80 runs with 0 dismissals cannot be calculated as 80 divided by zero. In scorecards, this may be shown as no average yet, infinite, or simply not applicable depending on the context.

## Average versus strike rate

Batting average measures consistency and survival. Strike rate measures scoring speed. A batter can have a high average but a low strike rate if they score slowly and rarely get out. Another batter can have a lower average but a high strike rate if they attack often.

Both numbers matter. In longer formats, average often receives more attention. In short formats, strike rate can be just as important because balls are limited.

## Use average with context

Averages can be misleading if the sample is tiny. One big innings can lift a batter's average early in a tournament. Batting position also matters. An opener may face the new ball, while a lower-order batter may face fewer balls but finish not out more often.

Pitch, match format, role, and opposition quality all matter too. Use average as a helpful summary, not as the only judgment.

## Practical scoring workflow

Keep a simple record of every innings: runs scored and whether the batter was out. Add all runs. Count only innings where the batter was dismissed. Then divide total runs by dismissals. The calculator can do the math quickly, but accurate input is still the key.
