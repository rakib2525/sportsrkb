## Step-by-step method

Start with the total number of legal balls. Divide that number by 6. The whole-number part is complete overs. The leftover balls become the balls part of the cricket notation.

Suppose you have 32 balls. Six balls fit into 32 five times, because 5 x 6 = 30. There are 2 balls left. That means 32 balls equals 5.2 overs.

This method works for any total. It is useful when you are reading ball-by-ball data, checking a scorebook, or using a calculator that needs overs in cricket format.

## Overs to balls

The reverse conversion is just as important. If you have 9.4 overs, multiply the complete overs by 6 and add the balls after the dot. That gives 9 x 6 + 4 = 58 balls.

If you have 15.0 overs, that is 15 x 6 = 90 balls. If you have 19.5 overs, that is 19 x 6 + 5 = 119 balls. One more legal ball would complete 20.0 overs.

## Why calculators care

Run rate and economy rate divide runs by overs. Required run rate divides runs needed by overs remaining. If the over value is converted incorrectly, the answer can be slightly wrong or very misleading in a close chase.

For example, 4.3 overs is 27 balls. As a decimal for division, that is 4.5 overs because 27 divided by 6 equals 4.5. Treating 4.3 as the decimal 4.3 would overstate the run rate.

## Common conversion table

- 6 balls = 1.0 over
- 12 balls = 2.0 overs
- 18 balls = 3.0 overs
- 24 balls = 4.0 overs
- 27 balls = 4.3 overs
- 30 balls = 5.0 overs

## Practical match use

In a 20-over chase, there are 120 legal balls. If 75 balls have been completed, 45 balls remain. That is 7.3 overs remaining. This helps a batting side understand both the required run rate and how many scoring opportunities are left.

The same idea helps bowlers. If a bowler has delivered 22 legal balls, they have bowled 3.4 overs. If they conceded 24 runs, their economy rate should use 22 balls converted safely, not a rough decimal guess.
