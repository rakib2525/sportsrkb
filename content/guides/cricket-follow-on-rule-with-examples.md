## What the follow-on means

The follow-on is a rule used in multi-innings cricket. If the team batting first takes a large enough first-innings lead, it may ask the opponent to bat again immediately instead of batting its own second innings next.

This rule is most associated with longer cricket because both teams can bat twice. It does not apply in the same way to ordinary limited-overs matches where each side bats once.

## The basic idea

Calculate the lead after both teams have completed their first innings. If Team A scores 400 and Team B scores 180, Team A leads by 220. In a 5-day match, that is normally enough for the follow-on option because the common threshold is 200.

The team with the lead does not have to enforce it. The rule gives an option, not an obligation.

## Common thresholds

A common set of follow-on thresholds is:

- 5-day match: 200 runs
- 4-day match: 150 runs
- 3-day match: 150 runs
- 2-day match: 100 runs

These thresholds are useful for learning and for simple calculators, but official playing conditions can vary. For any real competition, the match regulations should be checked.

## Example for a 5-day match

Team A scores 510. Team B scores 295. The lead is 215. In a 5-day match with a 200-run threshold, Team A can enforce the follow-on.

If Team B had scored 325, the lead would be 185. That is below 200, so the follow-on would not be available under the common 5-day threshold.

## Example for a 3-day match

Team A scores 280. Team B scores 125. The lead is 155. In a 3-day match with a 150-run threshold, the follow-on may be available.

Shorter multi-day matches use smaller thresholds because there is less scheduled playing time. The rule helps the leading team push for a result when time is limited.

## Why a captain may enforce it

Enforcing the follow-on can keep pressure on the batting side. If the opponent has just been bowled out cheaply, sending them back in may create another collapse. It can also save time because the leading team does not spend extra overs batting again.

This is especially tempting if weather or remaining time makes a result difficult.

## Why a captain may decline it

Captains sometimes choose not to enforce the follow-on. Bowlers may be tired after a long innings. The pitch may be expected to get worse later, making it useful to bat again and set a bigger target. The captain may also want to avoid chasing in the fourth innings.

So the follow-on is both a rule and a strategy decision.
