## Start with the team score

The fastest way to read a cricket scorecard is to start with the team total. A score such as 168/5 means the team scored 168 runs and lost 5 wickets. If the innings is complete, the overs will show how many overs were used.

In limited-overs cricket, a score might be 168/5 in 20.0 overs. In a chase, it might be 169/4 in 18.2 overs, meaning the team reached the target before using all overs.

## Understand wickets

The number after the slash is wickets lost. A team can lose up to 10 wickets in a normal innings. If a team is all out, the score may show 10 wickets lost even if all available overs were not used.

Wickets matter because they affect match strategy. A team scoring quickly with many wickets left is in a different position from a team scoring at the same pace with only one wicket remaining.

## Read overs correctly

Overs are written in cricket notation. 12.4 means 12 overs and 4 balls. It is not 12.4 decimal overs. This detail is essential when using calculators. If you need decimal overs, convert balls first.

For example, 12.4 overs equals 76 balls. Decimal overs are 76 divided by 6, or 12.6667. A run rate calculation should use the decimal value internally.

## Batting section

The batting section lists each batter, how they were dismissed, runs scored, balls faced, and often fours, sixes, and strike rate. Strike rate is runs per 100 balls. If a batter scores 50 from 40 balls, the strike rate is 125.00.

Not-out batters are marked because they were not dismissed. Their runs count for the team and for batting average, but the innings does not add a dismissal for average calculation.

## Bowling section

The bowling section shows overs, maidens, runs conceded, wickets, and economy rate. Some scorecards also show wides and no-balls. Economy rate is runs conceded per over.

If a bowler has figures of 4.0 overs, 24 runs, and 2 wickets, the economy is 6.00 and the bowler took two wickets. The bowling average for that match would be 12.00 if using only those figures.

## Extras section

Extras are runs not credited to a batter as runs off the bat. They can include wides, no-balls, byes, and leg byes. Extras are part of the team total, so a batting card may not add up to the total unless extras are included.

Understanding extras helps explain why a bowling spell may include more runs than the batters' shots suggest.

## Fall of wickets

Many scorecards include fall of wickets, showing the score when each wicket fell. This helps readers understand partnerships and momentum. For example, 20/2 suggests early trouble, while 140/2 suggests a strong platform.

Fall of wickets is useful when reviewing a chase or identifying where the match changed.

## Using scorecards with calculators

A scorecard gives the inputs for calculators. Use runs and overs for run rate, runs and balls for batting strike rate, runs conceded and overs for economy rate, and runs plus wickets for bowling average.

For NRR, you need team runs, overs faced, runs conceded, and overs bowled across the match or set of matches you want to estimate.

## Final reading tip

Do not read one number in isolation. A score of 150 may be strong on a difficult pitch and weak on a small ground. A batter's 30 from 20 balls may be excellent in a chase but less useful if the team needed faster scoring earlier.

A scorecard gives the facts. Good cricket understanding comes from connecting those facts to format, conditions, and match situation.
