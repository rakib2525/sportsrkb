## What extras are

Extras are runs that increase the team total without being credited to a batter as runs off the bat. They are part of cricket scoring because not every run comes from a normal batting shot. Some come from illegal deliveries, missed balls, or deflections off the batter's body.

The common extras are wides, no-balls, byes, and leg byes. Scorecards often show them in a separate extras line so readers can see how many runs were added outside the batting card.

## Wides

A wide is called when the ball is too far from the batter to reasonably reach and play, based on the match rules and umpire judgment. A wide usually adds one run to the batting team. If the ball reaches the boundary or batters run, more runs may be added as wides.

A wide is not a legal ball, so it does not reduce the number of balls left in the over. This is important for over conversion and bowling analysis. Wides are normally charged against the bowler.

## No-balls

A no-ball is an illegal delivery. It can happen for several reasons, such as overstepping the crease, bowling an illegal full toss, or breaking another delivery rule. A no-ball adds at least one run to the batting team and the delivery usually does not count as a legal ball.

Runs scored off the bat on a no-ball can count to the batter, while the no-ball extra is added separately. In many limited-overs formats, a no-ball may also lead to a free hit, depending on playing conditions.

## Byes

Byes happen when the ball passes the batter without touching the bat or body, and the batters run or the ball reaches the boundary. Bye runs are added to the team total but are not credited to the batter.

Byes are not normally charged to the bowler's runs conceded. They may reflect wicketkeeping difficulty, bounce, movement, or missed chances, but the exact interpretation depends on the situation.

## Leg byes

Leg byes happen when the ball touches the batter's body or protective equipment, not the bat, and the batters run or the ball reaches the boundary. The umpire must be satisfied that the batter attempted to play the ball or avoid being hit under the relevant law.

Leg byes are added to the team total, not to the batter's runs. Like byes, they are not normally charged to the bowler in the same way as runs off the bat.

## Extras and overs

Wides and no-balls usually do not count as legal deliveries. Byes and leg byes can occur on legal deliveries, so those balls usually count in the over. This distinction matters when calculating run rate, economy, dot ball percentage, and balls remaining.

If a bowler sends a wide and then a legal delivery, the over has still advanced by only one legal ball after the legal delivery.

## Extras and bowling figures

Wides and no-balls can hurt a bowler's figures because they add runs and often require extra deliveries. Too many extras can turn a good over into an expensive one. Byes and leg byes are part of the team total but are not usually counted against the bowler's conceded runs.

The bowler's spell analyzer can help track wides, no-balls, dot balls, boundaries, and economy, giving a fuller view of discipline and control.

## Practical example

Suppose a team total is 155. The batters' runs add up to 143. The remaining 12 runs are extras. The scorecard might show 5 wides, 2 no-balls, 3 byes, and 2 leg byes.

That line explains why the batting card does not equal the total by itself. It also highlights whether the bowling side gave away avoidable runs.

## Why beginners should learn extras

Extras affect totals, overs, bowling analysis, and match pressure. A close match can be decided by a few wides or no-balls. A chase can become easier when the bowling side loses discipline.

Once you understand extras, scorecards become much easier to read. You can see not only how many runs were scored, but how those runs were created.
