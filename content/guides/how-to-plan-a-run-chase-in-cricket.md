## Start with the target

Every chase starts with one simple question: how many runs are needed from how many balls? If the target is 181, the batting side needs 181 to win. If it is 60 after 8 overs in a 20-over match, it needs 121 from 72 balls.

That equation gives the required run rate. In this example, 121 runs from 12 overs requires 10.08 runs per over. This is a demanding chase, but not impossible if wickets are in hand.

## Check wickets in hand

Required run rate does not include wickets, but wickets strongly affect the plan. A team needing 80 from 48 balls with 8 wickets in hand can play differently from a team needing the same runs with 3 wickets left.

More wickets allow more risk. Fewer wickets usually require more careful shot selection. A collapse can turn a manageable required rate into a losing position.

## Break the chase into blocks

Large targets can feel overwhelming. Breaking the chase into blocks helps. Instead of thinking "we need 96 from 60 balls," a team can plan 32 from the next 4 overs, then 32 from the next 4, then reassess the final 4.

This approach keeps the batting side flexible. If one block goes better than expected, the next block can be calmer. If one block goes badly, the team can decide whether to attack earlier.

## Understand match phases

In T20 cricket, the powerplay, middle overs, and death overs often require different plans. The powerplay may offer scoring gaps because of fielding restrictions. The middle overs may require strike rotation. The death overs may demand boundaries.

In ODI cricket, the chase is longer. Teams may build steadily for 30 or 35 overs before accelerating. The required rate should be checked often, but panic too early can waste batting resources.

## Identify target overs

Most chases have overs where the batting side expects to score more. This might be against a part-time bowler, a shorter boundary, or a matchup that suits a batter. Good chase planning identifies these moments without relying on fake predictions.

The idea is not to attack blindly. It is to know when the best scoring opportunities are likely to appear and be ready to use them.

## Rotate strike

Boundaries are important, but singles and twos keep the required rate under control. Too many dot balls create pressure. A team can fall behind even without losing wickets if it allows several quiet overs.

Rotating strike also disrupts bowlers. It changes angles, prevents one batter from being tied down, and keeps both batters involved.

## Know when to change gears

A chase plan should not be fixed for the entire innings. If the required rate rises above a comfortable level, the batting side may need to take more risk. If wickets fall, the team may need to rebuild for a few overs.

Good chase planning is about timing. Attack too late and the target may become impossible. Attack too early and wickets may fall before the finish.

## Use calculators wisely

The required run rate calculator gives the current equation. The chase planner can show aggressive, balanced, and late-charge options. These tools help organize thinking, but they do not replace cricket judgment.

Use the numbers to guide decisions, then adjust based on conditions, player strengths, and the actual match situation.
