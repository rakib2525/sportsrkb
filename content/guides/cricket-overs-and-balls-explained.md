## Why overs matter

Overs control the rhythm of a cricket innings. A team in a 20-over match has 120 legal balls to use. A team in a 50-over match has 300 legal balls. Bowlers usually bowl in spells, batters plan phases around overs, and calculators use overs to work out scoring speed.

Because overs are so central, a small misunderstanding can change a calculation. If a player enters 4.3 as a decimal number, they may treat it as 4.3 overs. In cricket, 4.3 means 4 overs and 3 balls. That equals 27 legal balls, or 4.5 decimal overs for mathematical division.

## Legal and illegal deliveries

A legal delivery counts as one ball in the over. Most normal deliveries are legal. A wide or no-ball usually does not count as a legal ball, although it can add runs to the batting team's total. This is why an over can contain more than six actual deliveries from the bowler.

For example, a bowler may bowl six legal balls plus one wide. The score increases by at least one run for the wide, but the over still needs six legal balls to be complete. This is important when calculating economy rate, run rate, and balls remaining.

## Partial overs

Partial overs are written with a dot. The number before the dot is complete overs. The number after the dot is balls in the current over. Valid examples include 0.1, 2.5, 10.3, and 19.5. Invalid examples include 4.6, 8.7, and 12.9 because six balls complete the next over.

If a bowler has bowled 3.5 overs, they have delivered 23 legal balls. One more legal delivery completes the fourth over. If a batting side is 15.0 overs into an innings, it has faced 90 legal balls.

## Common mistakes

The most common mistake is treating cricket overs like normal decimals. Another mistake is writing 6 balls as `.6`. In cricket notation, the balls part never reaches 6. After 4.5 comes 5.0, not 4.6.

When using a calculator, always enter overs in cricket format if the tool asks for cricket overs. If the tool asks for total balls, enter a whole number of legal balls instead.

## Practical use

Overs and balls help answer everyday cricket questions. How many balls are left in a chase? What is the run rate after 8.2 overs? How many legal balls did a bowler deliver in 3.4 overs? Once you can convert between overs and balls, these questions become simple arithmetic.
