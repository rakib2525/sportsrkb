## Phase one: powerplay

The powerplay is usually the first six overs of a full T20 innings. Because fielding restrictions limit boundary protection, batting teams often try to score quickly. A strong powerplay can reduce pressure later, but losing too many wickets can slow the middle overs.

A team chasing 170 might be comfortable at 48 for 1 after six overs. The current run rate is 8.00, and the required rate for the remaining 14 overs is still manageable. The same score at 48 for 4 feels very different because the batting resources are weaker.

## Phase two: middle overs

Overs 7 to 15 often decide whether a T20 innings becomes ordinary or dangerous. Batters try to rotate strike, punish loose balls, and avoid long boundary droughts. Bowlers often use spin, cutters, or defensive fields to slow the rate.

In this phase, run rate should be read with wickets. A team at 90 for 2 after 12 overs may be ready to accelerate. A team at 90 for 6 may need to rebuild and accept a lower finish.

## Phase three: death overs

The final five overs are where run rate can change fastest. Boundaries, extras, and wickets all have a large effect. A team needing 50 from 30 balls requires 10 runs per over. That is difficult but possible with wickets in hand and boundary options.

Death-over strategy often uses matchups. Batters may target shorter boundaries or specific bowlers. Bowlers may use yorkers, slower balls, and wide lines to reduce boundary chances.

## Chasing strategy

In a chase, required run rate is more important than current run rate alone. A team may have a current rate of 7.50, but if the required rate is 11.00, it needs to accelerate soon.

Good chasing teams break the target into smaller blocks. Instead of thinking only about 90 from 60 balls, they may plan 35 from the next 4 overs, then reassess.

## Batting first strategy

Batting first requires judging par score. Without live data or fake predictions, the safe approach is to use cricket sense: pitch pace, boundary size, batting depth, and bowling quality. A team should aim for a total that creates pressure while still protecting against collapse.

## Simple takeaway

T20 run rate strategy is about timing risk. Attack when conditions and wickets allow it. Absorb pressure when needed. Keep checking the required rate, balls remaining, and wickets in hand.
