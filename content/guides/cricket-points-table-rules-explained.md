## Common table columns

A points table usually shows matches played, wins, losses, ties, no-results, points, and net run rate. Some tables also show runs for, overs faced, runs against, and overs bowled, but the public version is often simpler.

Matches played tells you how many games a team has completed. Points show the main ranking score. NRR gives extra separation when points are equal.

## Wins and losses

Many cricket tournaments award two points for a win and zero for a loss, but this is not universal. Some formats may use different values. A team with more wins usually rises quickly because points matter before tiebreakers.

Losses matter because they reduce the number of remaining chances. A team may still qualify with losses if other results help, but it usually needs enough points and a healthy tiebreak position.

## Ties and no-results

Ties and no-results can be handled differently. A tied match may be followed by a tiebreak method, or points may be shared. A no-result caused by weather may also lead to shared points.

Because these outcomes vary, any qualification calculation should include the tournament's actual points rules.

## Net run rate in tables

NRR is often used when teams are level on points. A positive NRR means a team has scored faster than it has conceded across counted matches. A negative NRR means the opposite.

NRR can reward big wins and punish heavy defeats. It is not a replacement for points, but it can become decisive in a tight group.

## Qualification zones

Tables often have a qualification zone at the top. The number of qualifying teams depends on the tournament format. A top-four race, top-two race, or group-winner system will all create different scenarios.

Generic scenario tools can help estimate projected points, but they are not official predictions. Real standings depend on actual match results and tournament rules.

## Safe way to read a table

First check points. Then check matches played, because teams may not have played the same number of games. Then check tiebreakers such as NRR. Finally, look at remaining matches to understand what is still possible.

## Manual planning example

Imagine a team has 10 points with three matches left, and each win is worth 2 points. Its maximum possible total is 16. If the likely qualification target is 14, the team may need two more wins. If it reaches exactly the target, NRR or another tiebreaker may become important.

This kind of planning is useful, but it should be described carefully. It is not a live prediction and it does not replace the official table. It simply helps readers understand what the remaining matches could mean.
