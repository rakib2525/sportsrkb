## Example 1: a comfortable chase

A team needs 48 runs from 12 overs. The required run rate is 48 divided by 12, which equals 4.00. This is usually comfortable in many limited-overs situations, especially if wickets are in hand.

But the number alone does not tell the whole story. A difficult pitch, strong bowling, or a long tail can make even a modest rate tricky. Required run rate should always be read with match context.

## Example 2: a manageable chase

A team needs 72 runs from 9 overs. The required run rate is 72 divided by 9, which equals 8.00. This may be manageable in T20 cricket but more demanding in a low-scoring match.

The batting side might plan to score around 25 from the next 3 overs, then reassess. Breaking the chase into smaller blocks makes the target feel less abstract.

## Example 3: pressure after quiet overs

Suppose a team needs 80 from 10 overs. The required rate is 8.00. If the next two overs bring only 8 runs, the equation becomes 72 from 8 overs. The new required rate is 9.00.

The team has not lost the chase, but pressure has increased. That is why dot balls and low-scoring overs matter during a chase.

## Example 4: late acceleration

A team needs 42 from 18 balls. There are 3 overs remaining, so the required run rate is 14.00. This is difficult because the batting side likely needs boundaries.

If the next over goes for 18, the equation becomes 24 from 12 balls. That is still 12.00 per over, but the chase feels more possible. A single big over can change the required rate quickly.

## Example 5: using balls instead of overs

Sometimes it is easier to think in balls. If a team needs 30 from 24 balls, that is 4 overs remaining. The required rate is 30 divided by 4, or 7.50.

If the team needs 30 from 18 balls, that is 3 overs. The rate becomes 10.00. The same runs needed can feel very different depending on balls remaining.

## Example 6: already reached target

If the target is 151 and the current score is 151 or more, the chase is already complete. A good calculator should show that no runs are needed instead of trying to divide by remaining overs.

Safe handling matters because real users may enter scores after a chase is complete.

## Example 7: no balls remaining

If overs completed equal total overs, there are no balls remaining. Required run rate cannot be calculated normally because there is no time left in the innings. A calculator should show a friendly message rather than crash.

This is especially important for manual score entry, where users may enter final innings figures.

## How to plan with required rate

Required run rate is a guide for tempo. If the rate is low, batters can rotate strike and avoid unnecessary risk. If it is rising, the batting side may need to target a bowler, boundary, or phase.

Use the required run rate calculator for the current equation, and use the chase planner when you want a phase-wise plan.
