## Why dismissals matter

Batting average divides total runs by the number of times a batter has been out. That is why not-out innings can raise or protect an average: the runs count, but the dismissal does not.

## Sample size matters

A batting average after two innings can swing wildly. Over more innings, the number usually becomes a more useful sign of consistency.

## Use with role and format

An opener, finisher, and lower-order batter may have different jobs. Average is useful, but it should be read beside strike rate, batting position, format, and match conditions.

## Not-outs and interpretation

Not-outs are one reason batting average can need context. A batter who finishes chases without being dismissed may have a strong average because their runs increase while dismissals do not. That can be a fair reflection of finishing skill, but sample size still matters.

For newer players or short tournaments, one not-out innings can change the number sharply. Over a larger set of innings, average becomes more stable and easier to compare.

## Practical comparison

Use batting average to understand consistency and strike rate to understand scoring speed. A batter with both a healthy average and useful strike rate is usually contributing in a balanced way.
