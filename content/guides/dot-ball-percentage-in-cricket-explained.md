## Why dot balls matter

Dot balls are small moments that add up. In a T20 innings, two quiet overs can change the required rate quickly. In ODIs, a long spell of dots can force batters to take risks later.

Bowlers use dot balls to build pressure. Batters feel that pressure because every ball used without scoring leaves fewer opportunities. This is why a bowler can have a strong impact even without taking many wickets.

## How to calculate it

Count legal balls bowled. Count how many of those legal balls produced no run. Divide dot balls by legal balls and multiply by 100.

If a bowler has 10 dot balls from 20 legal balls, the dot ball percentage is 50%. If a team faces 60 legal balls and has 25 dots, the innings dot ball percentage is 41.67%.

## Dot balls and economy

Dot ball percentage often connects with economy rate, but the two numbers are not identical. A bowler can bowl many dots but still concede boundaries. Another bowler may concede steady singles but few boundaries.

For example, an over with four dots and two sixes costs 12 runs. Another over with six singles costs 6 runs. The first over has a higher dot ball count, but the second over has a better economy.

## Reading the number safely

A high dot ball percentage is usually good for a bowler, but context matters. A bowler operating with defensive fields may allow easy singles. A death bowler may have a lower dot percentage because batters swing hard every ball.

Dot balls are most useful when read with boundaries conceded, wickets, extras, and economy rate.

## Batting perspective

For batters, reducing dot balls can be as important as hitting boundaries. Rotating strike keeps the innings moving and prevents the required rate from climbing. In a chase, turning dots into singles can make the final overs easier.

## Practical use

Use dot ball percentage to analyze a spell or innings. It helps explain pressure that may not appear in wickets alone. A bowler who bowls 14 dots in 4 overs has controlled more than half their legal balls, which can be a major contribution.
