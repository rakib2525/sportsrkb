## Why rain changes cricket

Cricket depends on overs, playing time, pitch condition, and safe weather. Rain can interrupt any of these. If a match loses time, organizers may reduce overs. If too much time is lost, the match may become a no-result.

Because cricket scoring is based on limited resources, rain can change the balance of a match. A team planning for 20 overs may suddenly have only 12. A chase target may need to be revised. Bowlers may have different over limits. Batters may need to change tempo immediately.

## Reduced-over matches

In many limited-overs matches, rain does not automatically end the game. If enough time remains, the match can be shortened. A T20 may become 15 overs per side. An ODI may become 38 overs per side. The exact minimum overs and adjustment rules depend on the competition.

Reduced-over matches make run rate more urgent. A score that was average for 20 overs may become strong for 12 overs, or the chase may be recalculated under a formal method. Manual calculators can help with simple run rate questions, but official revised targets must come from the match rules.

## No-results

If a match cannot reach the minimum required overs, it may be declared a no-result. In many tournaments, teams share points for a no-result. But this is not universal, so the points table rules should be checked.

For local tournaments, decide before the event how many points are awarded for no-results. A common simple structure is win 2, no-result 1, loss 0. That is easy to manage in a manual points table.

## Rain and NRR

Rain can make NRR confusing. If a match is shortened, official NRR rules may use adjusted overs or revised targets. If a match is abandoned as a no-result, it may not affect NRR at all. The exact handling depends on playing conditions.

This is why a manual NRR calculator should be treated as an estimate in rain-affected situations. It can explain the math, but it may not reproduce official table values.

## Practical local tournament advice

For school, club, or friendly tournaments, write down rain rules before the first match. Include minimum overs, points for no-results, whether reserve time exists, and how NRR is handled for shortened matches.

Clarity is more important than complexity. A simple rule that everyone understands is better than a complicated rule that creates arguments later.

## Chase planning after rain

When overs are reduced during a chase, batters need to know the new target, balls remaining, and required rate. The required run rate calculator can help once the official revised target is known.

Do not invent a target or use fake data. Use the target given by match officials or tournament rules, then calculate the chase equation from that point.

## Points table impact

Rain can change qualification scenarios. Shared points may help one team and hurt another. A no-result late in a group stage can make NRR more important if teams finish level.

Use the points table calculator to update standings manually after the official result is known. Keep a note of rain-affected results so teams understand why points or NRR changed.
