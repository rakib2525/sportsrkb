## Why statistics matter

Cricket has a lot of numbers, but beginners do not need to learn everything at once. The most important statistics help answer simple questions. How fast is the team scoring? How quickly is a batter scoring? How controlled is a bowler? How is a team ranked in a tournament?

Once you understand those questions, scorecards and calculators become much easier to use.

## Runs, wickets, and overs

The basic score is runs for wickets in overs. A score of 145/6 in 20 overs means the team scored 145 runs and lost 6 wickets. Overs show how much of the innings has been used.

Wickets matter because they show batting resources. A team with 80/1 is usually in a different position from a team with 80/7, even if the runs and overs are the same.

## Run rate

Run rate shows team scoring speed. The formula is runs divided by decimal overs. If a team scores 72 in 9 overs, the run rate is 8.00.

Run rate is useful in both innings. Batting first, it helps estimate scoring pace. Chasing, it can be compared with required run rate.

## Required run rate

Required run rate is used in a chase. It shows how many runs per over the batting team needs from the current point. If a team needs 48 from 6 overs, the required rate is 8.00.

This number changes after every ball. A boundary can lower it. A quiet over can raise it.

## Batting strike rate

Batting strike rate measures runs per 100 balls. If a batter scores 30 from 20 balls, the strike rate is 150.00. It is a scoring-speed stat for individual batters.

Strike rate is especially important in limited-overs cricket, but context matters. A slow innings may be useful if the pitch is difficult or wickets have fallen.

## Batting average

Batting average measures runs per dismissal. The formula is total runs divided by dismissals. Not-out innings count in runs but not in dismissals.

Average is more useful over several innings than one match. A small sample can mislead.

## Economy rate

Economy rate measures how many runs a bowler concedes per over. If a bowler gives 24 runs in 4 overs, the economy is 6.00.

Economy is a control stat. It does not tell you everything about wickets, but it shows whether the bowler kept scoring down.

## Bowling average and strike rate

Bowling average is runs conceded per wicket. Bowling strike rate is balls per wicket. Lower values are usually better for both, but roles matter.

A bowler can have a good economy without many wickets, or take wickets while conceding runs. Reading several bowling stats together gives a clearer picture.

## Net run rate

Net run rate compares a team's scoring rate with the rate it concedes. It is often used in tournament tables when teams finish level on points. Positive NRR usually means the team has scored faster than opponents across counted matches.

NRR can be tricky because overs and all-out innings may have tournament-specific rules. Use calculators for estimates and official rules for final standings.

## Extras and dot balls

Extras are runs not credited to batters as normal bat runs, such as wides, no-balls, byes, and leg byes. Dot balls are deliveries where no run is scored. Both can influence pressure.

Too many extras hurt the bowling side. Too many dot balls hurt the batting side.

## How to learn faster

Pick one scorecard and calculate three numbers: team run rate, one batter's strike rate, and one bowler's economy. Then read the match situation. Were wickets falling? Was the team chasing? Was the pitch difficult?

Statistics are most useful when they help tell the cricket story, not when they replace it.
