## Why it matters in a chase

Required run rate helps players and fans understand the pace needed from the remaining balls. It is especially useful late in an innings, when every over used without enough runs makes the target harder.

## Reading the number

A required rate below the current scoring rate usually feels manageable. A rate much higher than the current scoring rate may require more boundaries, riskier running, or a change in batting approach.

## Planning safely

The number is a guide, not a prediction. Batting depth, wickets in hand, bowling quality, field settings, and pitch behavior can all change the practical difficulty.

## Chase planning blocks

A useful way to read required run rate is to break the chase into blocks. Instead of only thinking about 84 from 60 balls, a team might plan 30 from the next 4 overs, then reassess. This keeps the chase flexible.

If the required rate is climbing, the batting side may need to target a specific over or bowler. If the rate is comfortable, singles and low-risk boundaries may be enough. The calculator gives the number; match judgment decides the approach.
