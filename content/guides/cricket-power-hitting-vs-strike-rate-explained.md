## What power hitting means

Power hitting usually refers to a batter's ability to hit boundaries, especially sixes and hard fours. It is a skill that can change a match quickly. A few clean hits can lift the scoring rate, reduce required run rate, and put pressure on bowlers.

But power hitting is not the same as strike rate. It is one way to create a high strike rate, not the only way.

## What strike rate means

Batting strike rate is a scorecard metric. It measures runs scored per 100 balls. The formula is runs divided by balls, multiplied by 100. If a batter scores 45 from 30 balls, the strike rate is 150.00.

Strike rate includes every run. A single, two, four, six, and even slow accumulation all affect the number. It does not care whether the runs looked powerful or gentle.

## Boundary-heavy innings

A power hitter may score quickly through boundaries. For example, a batter might score 36 from 18 balls with four sixes and two singles. That is a strike rate of 200.00. The innings has high boundary impact and high scoring speed.

This style can be valuable at the end of an innings or when the required rate is rising. The risk is that missed power shots can bring wickets.

## Rotation-heavy innings

Another batter might score 45 from 35 balls with few sixes but many singles, twos, and fours. The strike rate is 128.57. It is lower than the power-hitting example, but it may be ideal on a difficult pitch or during a rebuild.

Rotating strike also prevents bowlers from settling. It can support a power hitter at the other end.

## Dot balls are the hidden issue

Power hitting can hide dot-ball problems. A batter who hits two sixes in an over but plays four dots scores 12 from 6 balls, which is still a strike rate of 200 for that over. But if the big hits do not come, dot balls create pressure quickly.

Great short-format batting often combines boundary options with low dot-ball percentage. The best innings are not only about hitting hard. They are about scoring regularly.

## Match role matters

An opener, anchor, finisher, and lower-order hitter may all have different strike-rate expectations. A finisher may face fewer balls and needs immediate impact. An opener may need to balance fielding restrictions with wicket protection.

This is why comparing strike rates without roles can be misleading. A batter's job in the innings matters.

## Strike rate and fantasy points

Generic fantasy scoring may include runs, boundaries, wickets, catches, and bonuses depending on rules. Power hitting can help through fours and sixes, while strike rate may matter in some systems. RKBSports uses generic examples only, because actual platform rules can differ.

Always check the rules of your contest or local scoring system before making decisions.

## Practical analysis

Use the strike rate calculator for the basic number. Then read the innings: balls faced, boundaries, dot balls, target, and wickets. A high strike rate in a losing cause may still be excellent. A lower strike rate in a successful chase may be exactly what the team needed.

Power hitting is exciting. Strike rate is measurable. Smart analysis respects both.
