## Dot balls and pressure

A dot ball is a legal delivery where the batting side scores no run. On its own, one dot ball may not seem important. Across an innings, dot balls can shape the entire match. They reduce scoring opportunities, make batters feel stuck, and force the required run rate upward.

In a 20-over innings, there are only 120 legal balls. If a team faces 45 dot balls, more than one-third of its innings has produced no runs. That means the scoring burden shifts heavily to the remaining balls.

## Impact on required run rate

Dot balls are especially important in a chase. Suppose a team needs 60 from 36 balls. That is 10 runs per over. If the next over has four dots and only two singles, the equation becomes 58 from 30 balls. The required rate rises to 11.60.

The batting side has not lost a wicket, but the chase has become harder. This is how dot balls create pressure without appearing dramatic.

## How bowlers use dot balls

Bowlers use dot balls to control tempo. A tight line, smart field, change of pace, or good length can make scoring difficult. If a batter cannot rotate strike, they may try a risky boundary shot.

This is why a bowler can bowl an excellent spell without taking many wickets. A spell with many dots can help another bowler take wickets later because the pressure has already been built.

## Dot balls and economy rate

Dot balls often support a good economy rate, but they do not guarantee it. An over with four dot balls and two sixes costs 12 runs. An over with six singles costs 6 runs. The first over has more dots, but the second over is cheaper.

That is why dot balls should be read alongside boundaries conceded, extras, and total runs. The bowler's spell analyzer is useful because it combines these factors.

## Batting response

Batters try to reduce dot-ball pressure by rotating strike. Singles and twos are valuable because they keep the scoreboard moving and prevent bowlers from settling into one plan.

A batter who cannot find singles may become predictable. Fielders can stay in catching positions, bowlers can repeat pressure lines, and the required rate can climb.

## Dot balls by format

In T20 cricket, dot balls are very expensive because the innings is short. In ODI cricket, teams can absorb more dots early, but long quiet periods still create pressure. In longer formats, dot balls can be part of defensive batting, so interpretation changes.

Always read dot-ball impact with match format. A maiden over in T20 is huge. A maiden in a long-format match may be less dramatic but still useful in building pressure.

## Fielding and dots

Good fielding turns possible singles into dots. A sharp point fielder, quick midwicket, or alert cover can stop rotation. Saving one run may not feel big, but repeated saves change the over.

Fielding also supports bowling plans. A bowler can attack a line more confidently when fielders protect the right scoring areas.

## Practical takeaway

Dot balls are pressure currency. They do not always show up as wickets, but they influence decisions, run rate, and chase difficulty. For match analysis, track dot balls along with economy, wickets, boundaries, and extras.
