## First 10 overs

The first 10 overs often set the base. The ball may move more, and fielding restrictions can create scoring chances. Batters usually want to avoid early collapse while still taking boundaries when available.

A score of 55 for 1 after 10 overs can be a useful start. A score of 70 for 4 may look faster, but the lost wickets can reduce scoring power later. ODI strategy rewards both scoring and survival.

## Overs 11 to 35

The middle overs are the heart of ODI batting. Teams rotate strike, target weaker overs, and try to keep wickets for the final push. A long quiet period can make the death overs too difficult.

If a team is 150 after 30 overs and wants 300, it needs 150 from the final 20 overs. That is 7.50 per over. With wickets in hand, it may be possible. With only a few wickets left, the same requirement becomes harder.

## Overs 36 to 50

The final 15 overs are usually about acceleration. Batters take more risks, and bowlers try to protect boundaries. Run rate can rise quickly if set batters are in.

A team at 210 after 40 overs needs 90 from the last 10 to reach 300. That requires 9.00 per over, which is a demanding but reachable finish in good batting conditions.

## Chasing in ODIs

ODI chases are often planned in blocks. A team may aim to stay close to the required rate for 30 overs, then use wickets in hand late. If the required rate climbs too high, the batting side may need to attack earlier.

The required run rate calculator is useful because it updates the chase equation: target, current score, overs completed, and total overs.

## Batting first in ODIs

When batting first, teams estimate a competitive total. The ideal run rate depends on pitch, outfield, boundary size, and bowling quality. Without live data, a simple planning method is to set phase goals and reassess every 10 overs.

## Key lesson

ODI run rate strategy is about building. A team needs enough scoring pace to avoid late panic, but enough control to keep wickets for the finish.
