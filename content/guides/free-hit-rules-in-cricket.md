## When a free hit is awarded

Free hits are most commonly seen in limited-overs cricket after a front-foot no-ball. Some competitions may also apply free hits after other no-ball types, so official playing conditions matter.

## Field changes

The fielding side may be restricted in how it changes the field for the free hit. If the batters crossed or a different batter is on strike, some changes may be allowed. The purpose is to stop the fielding side from gaining an unfair advantage after the no-ball.

## Why free hits change tactics

Because the batter cannot be dismissed in most normal ways, they often look for a boundary. Bowlers usually aim for a safer delivery, such as a yorker or wide line, while avoiding another no-ball.

## Scoring impact in an innings

A free hit can change the run rate quickly. The no-ball already adds a penalty run, and the extra delivery gives the batting side another scoring chance. If the free hit goes for a boundary, the over can become expensive very quickly.

For bowlers, the priority is control. A legal delivery that limits the batter to one or two runs can recover some pressure. Another no-ball or wide can extend the damage.
