## Why death bowling is hard

Death overs are high-pressure because batters usually have license to attack. Wickets may matter less than quick runs. A batter might swing at almost every ball if only a few overs remain.

For bowlers, the margin for error is small. A missed yorker can become a full toss. A predictable slower ball can be hit over the boundary. A wide or no-ball gives away runs and an extra delivery.

## Yorker strategy

The yorker is one of the most famous death-over deliveries. It aims at the batter's feet or base of the stumps, making it hard to get under the ball. A well-executed yorker can prevent boundaries.

But yorkers are difficult to bowl under pressure. If the length is slightly wrong, the ball can become a low full toss or half-volley. That is why bowlers need practice and confidence before relying on yorkers every ball.

## Slower balls

Slower balls work by disrupting timing. Batters often prepare for pace at the death. If the ball arrives slower than expected, the shot can be mistimed.

The best slower balls are disguised. If the batter sees the change early, they can wait and hit cleanly. Bowlers may use back-of-the-hand slower balls, cutters, or pace-off deliveries depending on skill.

## Wide lines

Some bowlers aim wide outside off stump with fielders protecting the boundary. This can make it harder for the batter to hit straight. However, the line must stay legal. Too wide becomes a wide and adds pressure on the bowler.

Wide yorkers and wide slower balls can be effective when matched with the right field.

## Hard length

Not every death ball has to be full. A hard length into the pitch can be useful if the surface is slow or the boundary square is protected. It can force batters to hit across the line or generate their own pace.

Hard length is risky on true pitches where batters can pull or flat-bat the ball. Like every strategy, it depends on conditions.

## Field setting

Death bowling works best with a clear field. If the plan is wide yorkers, protect deep point, cover, and third areas. If the plan is straight yorkers, protect long-on and long-off. A mismatch between ball plan and field creates easy scoring options.

Captains and bowlers should agree on the plan before the run-up. Confusion at the death is expensive.

## Extras control

Wides and no-balls are damaging in death overs because they add runs and extra balls. A no-ball can also create a free hit in many limited-overs playing conditions. One mistake can change the over.

Good death bowlers often value legal execution first. A single is better than a wide plus another delivery.

## Measuring death bowling

Economy rate matters, but context matters too. Conceding 10 per over at the death may be acceptable in a very high-scoring match. Conceding 10 per over when defending a small target may be costly.
