## T20 powerplay

The T20 powerplay usually covers overs 1 to 6. Batting teams often aim for a strong start because the fielding side has fewer boundary riders available.

## ODI powerplay

ODI cricket uses fielding restriction phases across a longer innings. The exact details can change with rule updates and competition conditions, but the core idea is that the number of fielders outside the inner circle is controlled.

## Tactical balance

Attacking too hard can bring wickets, while being too cautious can leave too much work for later overs. Good powerplay batting balances boundary options with wicket preservation.

## Bowling plans in the powerplay

Bowlers often aim for early wickets because new batters can slow the scoring rate. Swing, seam movement, hard lengths, and changes of pace can all be used depending on conditions. The fielding side wants to avoid easy boundaries while still creating catching chances.

In T20 cricket, one strong powerplay can shape the entire innings. In ODI cricket, the first phase matters too, but teams have more time to recover from a slow start.

## Measuring the phase

Powerplay run rate is a simple way to compare starts. Divide powerplay runs by powerplay overs. Then judge the number with wickets lost and match conditions, not in isolation.
