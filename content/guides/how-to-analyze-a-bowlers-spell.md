## Start with the role

Before judging a bowler's spell, ask what the bowler was asked to do. A new-ball bowler may be expected to attack. A middle-over spinner may be asked to control runs. A death bowler may face batters trying to hit every ball.

The same figures can mean different things in different roles. Three overs for 21 with one wicket might be good in a high-scoring death phase and ordinary in a quiet middle phase.

## Economy rate

Economy rate shows runs conceded per over. It is the easiest way to judge run control. If a bowler concedes 28 from 4 overs, the economy is 7.00.

Economy is important, but it can be misleading alone. A bowler may concede a few runs while never threatening wickets. Another may concede more but dismiss key batters.

## Wickets

Wickets change matches. A spell with wickets can break partnerships, slow scoring, and expose weaker batters. However, wickets should still be read with context. A bowler may take a wicket from a poor shot created by scoreboard pressure built by another bowler.

This does not reduce the value of wickets. It simply means analysis should not stop there.

## Dot ball percentage

Dot balls are deliveries where no run is scored off the bat or extras. A high dot ball percentage builds pressure. In short formats, dots can force batters to take risks.

If a bowler delivers 24 legal balls and 12 are dots, the dot ball percentage is 50 percent. That is a useful sign of control, especially if boundaries are limited.

## Boundaries conceded

A bowler can have a decent economy while still releasing pressure through boundaries at key times. Counting fours and sixes conceded helps show whether expensive deliveries were frequent or isolated.

Boundary frequency is also useful for death bowling review. A bowler may accept singles but must reduce repeated boundaries.

## Extras and discipline

Wides and no-balls hurt a spell because they add runs and often force the bowler to deliver extra balls. In local cricket, extras can decide close matches. Tracking them helps identify control issues.

Byes and leg byes are different because they are not usually charged to the bowler in the same way. When reviewing a spell, separate bowling extras from fielding or wicketkeeping extras when possible.

## Match phase context

Powerplay, middle overs, and death overs create different challenges. In the powerplay, field restrictions can make boundaries easier. In the middle overs, batters may rotate strike. At the death, risk and scoring intent increase.

A fair spell analysis compares the bowler's figures with the phase and match situation.

## Use the analyzer

The bowler's spell analyzer combines economy, dots, boundaries, extras, and wickets into a clearer review. It is not a perfect ranking system, but it helps beginners see more than one number.

For better learning, write a short note beside the figures: pitch condition, batters faced, phase, and whether the bowler executed the plan.

## Practical local cricket review

For a local team, review the spell soon after the match while details are still fresh. Ask whether the bowler forced batters into low-risk singles, beat the bat, protected one side of the field, or missed the plan with too many loose deliveries. A short written review beside the numbers is often more useful than a long argument about one over.

If the same problem appears across several matches, such as too many wides or repeated boundaries in one area, the team has something specific to practice. That is the real value of spell analysis: turning scorecard numbers into better preparation.
