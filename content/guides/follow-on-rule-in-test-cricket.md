## Why the rule exists

The follow-on can help a dominant team push for a result. Instead of batting again immediately, the leading team can ask the trailing team to bat a second time and try to win by an innings.

## Tactical risks

Enforcing the follow-on can tire the bowling attack. If the trailing team bats much better in its second innings, the leading team may still need to chase a target late in the match.

## Match length matters

The commonly discussed 200-run threshold applies to five-day cricket. Shorter first-class matches can use different thresholds, so it is best to check the playing conditions for the match format.

## Captaincy decision

Even when the threshold is met, enforcing the follow-on is a choice. A captain may enforce it to push for an innings win or take advantage of tired opposition batting. A captain may decline it to rest bowlers, avoid batting last, or let the pitch deteriorate further before the opponent's final innings.

Weather can also affect the decision. If time is short, enforcing the follow-on may be more attractive. If there is plenty of time left, batting again can make the target safer.
