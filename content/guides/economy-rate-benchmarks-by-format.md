## Why benchmarks are tricky

Economy rate is simple to calculate but not always simple to judge. A bowler conceding 7 runs per over may be excellent in one match and average in another. The format, pitch, boundary size, ball condition, and batting approach all matter.

That is why fixed economy benchmarks can mislead. It is better to compare a bowler's economy with the match scoring rate and the role they were asked to perform.

## T20 economy

T20 cricket usually has the highest scoring pressure. Batters know they have only 120 legal balls, so they attack more often. Fielding restrictions, short boundaries, and power hitters can make economy control difficult.

In T20, a bowler who stays below the innings scoring rate has often done a useful job. A middle-over bowler may be judged differently from a death bowler. Conceding 7 per over in the middle overs can be strong. Conceding 9 at the death may still be acceptable in the right context.

## ODI economy

ODI cricket is longer, so batters often build through phases. Economy rates may be lower than T20 because there is more time to rebuild after wickets. However, final overs in ODIs can still be expensive.

A new-ball bowler may aim to take wickets and keep the rate controlled. A middle-over bowler may try to reduce singles and force risk. A death bowler may focus on preventing boundaries even if singles are conceded.

## Long-format economy

In long-format cricket, economy is still useful but has a different meaning. A bowler can create pressure by bowling maidens and forcing batters to defend for long periods. Wickets are often more important than run control alone.

A low economy in long-format cricket can support attacking fields because the batting side is not scoring freely. But a bowler also needs wicket threat, especially when trying to win a match.

## Phase matters

Powerplay economy, middle-over economy, and death-over economy should not be judged the same way. The powerplay may have fielding restrictions. Middle overs may have more boundary protection. Death overs usually involve maximum batting risk.

A fair benchmark asks: when did the bowler bowl, who was batting, what field was allowed, and what was the match situation?

## Role matters

Some bowlers are picked to attack. Others are picked to control. A strike bowler may concede more runs while taking wickets. A defensive bowler may take fewer wickets but reduce scoring pressure.

Neither role is automatically better. Team balance decides what is needed.

## Practical benchmark method

Instead of memorizing one number, use three comparisons. Compare the bowler's economy to the team economy. Compare it to the match run rate. Compare it to the expected role and phase.

If a bowler is below the match rate, limits boundaries, and avoids extras, the spell is usually useful. If the bowler is above the match rate and concedes frequent boundaries, the spell may need improvement.

## Use with other metrics

Economy rate works best with dot ball percentage, wickets, boundary frequency, and bowling average. The bowler's spell analyzer brings these ideas together for a broader view.

The goal is not to label every economy number as good or bad. The goal is to understand what the bowler contributed in context.
