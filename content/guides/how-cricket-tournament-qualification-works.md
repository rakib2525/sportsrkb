## Points are usually first

Most cricket tables rank teams by points before anything else. Wins usually add points. Losses usually add none. Ties and no-results may add shared points depending on the tournament rules.

This means the first question is simple: how many points can a team reach? If a team has 8 points and four matches left, and each win is worth 2 points, the maximum possible total is 16.

## Remaining matches matter

A team with fewer points may still be in a good position if it has more matches remaining. A team with more points may still be at risk if rivals have easier paths or more games left.

Qualification scenarios should always consider current points, matches remaining, points per win, and expected wins. Even then, they are only estimates because other results matter.

## Target points

Fans often talk about a target number of points. This is a rough qualification line, not a guarantee. In one tournament, 12 points may be enough. In another, teams may need more because many results cluster together.

A target points number is useful for planning. It helps answer: how many wins are still needed? If the target is 14 and a team has 10, it needs 4 more points. With 2 points per win, that means two more wins.

## Net run rate as a tiebreaker

If multiple teams finish on the same points, NRR may become important. A team with a positive NRR can have an advantage over teams with weaker NRR. But NRR is not always the first tiebreaker, so rules must be checked.

Improving NRR can be useful, especially late in a group stage. Still, chasing points should normally come first. A risky attempt to improve NRR can backfire if it costs the match.

## Other teams affect the picture

Qualification is not only about one team. If rival teams keep winning, the target may rise. If rivals lose, the path may become easier. This is why scenario tools should be treated as planning aids, not promises.

## Practical approach

Track current points, possible maximum points, minimum wins needed, and NRR. Then compare those numbers with the likely qualification zone. Keep the language cautious: likely, possible, difficult, or already safe. Avoid fake predictions or invented standings.
